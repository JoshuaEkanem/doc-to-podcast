Hi everyone, welcome to today's episode. We're going to be talking about the growing challenge of securing our Internet of Things devices in Nigeria.

The Internet of Things has grown at an incredible pace, with billions of smart devices collecting and exchanging data across interconnected networks. This is creating a huge cybersecurity challenge for organisations that are adopting IoT technology. 

In Nigeria, we've seen widespread adoption of IoT in industries like banking, healthcare, logistics, telecommunications, and even smart infrastructure. The digital economy is driving this growth, but it's also exposing these systems to new security risks.

One of the major concerns is edge computing - processing data closer to where it's generated can improve latency and responsiveness, but it also introduces more attack surfaces that can multiply the security risks of traditional architectures.

Most IoT devices are developed with minimal security mechanisms, making them vulnerable to hacking. We're talking about unauthorised access, weak authentication, insecure communication protocols, data interception, and privacy breaches - all these are major concerns for organisations in Nigeria.

But what's even more concerning is that there isn't a tool out there that can directly map IoT security findings to the requirements of our data protection laws. In this case, the Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023.

That's why researchers at Miva Open University have developed an NDPA-compliant IoT edge vulnerability assessment system. They built it using Python, Flask, Nmap, SQLite, and ReportLab - quite a tech stack!

The system has four key capabilities: multi-method device discovery, rule-based vulnerability detection across 18 ports that are relevant to IoT security, privacy risk assessment mapped to six principles of the NDPA, and automated compliance report generation.

They put their system to the test on a live MiFi network - which consisted of four devices, including a MiFi router and an Android-based car headunit. What they found was promising: one medium-severity vulnerability finding that correctly mapped to the NDPA, six privacy risk findings spanning multiple sections of the Act, and a network privacy score of 3.1/10.

But here's the best part - their system is easy to use and doesn't require any specialist configuration. This means that organisations in Nigeria can now have access to an NDPA-aligned IoT compliance assessment tool that's accessible to non-specialist compliance officers.
